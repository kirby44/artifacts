Our Mission

We're a thriving special interest group for AI safety in Japan.

Our mission is to get academics, students and professionals in Japan thinking about and working in AI safety, whether through technical alignment research, developing good policies for AI governance, or otherwise.

The Opportunity

Japan has the standing to shape global AI governance.

We aim to address an urgent inefficiency in the current landscape: the shortage of people from Japan positioned to positively influence the trajectory of advanced AI development.

Japan possesses the political and diplomatic weight to shape international AI governance. Under Japan's G7 presidency in May 2023, Japan led the launch of the Hiroshima AI Process — the G7 as a body had not previously played such a significant role in international AI governance, but did so under Japanese leadership. Just seven months later, this produced the Hiroshima AI Process Comprehensive Policy Framework — the world's first international framework for governing generative AI, anchored by international guiding principles and a code of conduct for frontier developers that has since expanded beyond the G7 to the Global South.1

Speaking of technical talent alone: Japan produces roughly 550,000 STEM graduates per year, and its business sector employs about 75% of the country's researchers — among the highest shares in the OECD, reflecting a deep, world-class engineering base.2

Yet global capacity in technical AI safety and governance remains critically limited. We see a massive structural bottleneck in the local ecosystem: virtually none of this top-percentile talent is funneled to AGI safety. Instead, this hidden reserve of technical experts flows almost exclusively into traditional industry roles, simply because they lack the context and infrastructure to apply their skills to AGI safety.

Compounding this, Japan's government spends only USD 8,184 per tertiary student versus the OECD average of USD 15,102, leaving little institutional slack to seed an entirely new field on its own.3 AIST exists to close this gap — to build the context, infrastructure, and community that turn latent talent into AI safety contribution.

What We Do

Building the basic infrastructure of a field where almost none existed.

Since January 2023, AIST has run a sustained, technically serious seminar series out of central Tokyo — over one hundred sessions across 40+ months, each organized around a single paper, report, or policy question, and conducted in English to serve as a meeting point for international and Japan-based researchers alike. Around that core we build the wider scaffolding a national field needs:

  • SeminarsRoughly weekly-to-monthly study sessions spanning mechanistic interpretability, alignment, capabilities forecasting, and AI governance.
  • ConferencesCo-organizing the international Technical AI Safety (TAIS) conferences, connecting Japan-based researchers with leading international groups.
  • TrainingBringing accelerators and structured programs to Japan to help talented people build the skills the field needs.

Theory of Change

From fragmentation to a coherent community.

Today Japan's AI safety landscape is fragmented — talented people, organizations, and resources exist, but they are scattered and disconnected. Our work is to weave them into a coherent community: one where people can learn from each other, communicate across the technical and policy divide, and collaborate on problems too large for any individual or organization alone. Each seminar, conference, and program is a thread in that fabric.
